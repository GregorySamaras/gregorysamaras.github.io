How Much Enterprise Data Should AI Be Allowed to Access?

May 2026 · Opinion

AI assistants are quickly becoming part of everyday work. Employees increasingly expect to ask questions in natural language and receive immediate answers from documents, emails, meetings, chat conversations, and internal knowledge bases.

From a productivity perspective, the appeal is obvious. The challenge is that access to information has always been one of the most important security controls inside an organisation.

A useful assistant or an information gateway?

Imagine an employee preparing for a customer meeting. Instead of searching through SharePoint sites, Teams conversations, emails, and project documentation, they simply ask an AI assistant:

"What are the key risks, open actions, and customer concerns related to this account?"

Within seconds, the assistant provides a concise summary assembled from multiple systems across the organisation.

From a productivity perspective, this is exactly the outcome many organisations want. The question is where the information came from, whether the employee should have had access to all of it, and whether the answer revealed relationships between data that would otherwise have remained separate.

The real question

Discussions around enterprise AI are often framed as technical challenges.

Can the platform be secured? Can access be controlled? Can sensitive information be protected?

These questions matter, but they are not the most important ones.

The more fundamental question is whether the AI should have access to the information in the first place. Once an assistant can search across organisational knowledge, summarise content, and identify relationships between documents, traditional boundaries around information become less obvious.

Why organisations want broad access

There are compelling reasons for giving AI systems access to large volumes of organisational data.

Employees spend significant amounts of time searching for information that already exists. Knowledge is often fragmented across departments, platforms, and individual employees. AI has the potential to reduce this friction, improve collaboration, and make institutional knowledge more accessible.

In many cases, the information already exists and the employee already has permission to access it. The AI simply makes discovery faster and more efficient.

What could go wrong?

Information access is rarely as simple as a permissions matrix.

Sensitive information can exist in unexpected places. Commercial negotiations, HR discussions, security investigations, legal reviews, and executive conversations often intersect with operational data in ways that are not immediately obvious.

An employee may not intentionally search for restricted information, yet an AI-generated summary could surface context that would have been difficult to discover through traditional search methods.

Organisations must also consider transparency and trust. Employees may reasonably ask what information is being analysed, how it is being processed, and who ultimately remains accountable for decisions influenced by AI-generated recommendations.

Security versus usefulness

An AI assistant that cannot access anything useful provides little value.

An AI assistant that can access everything introduces risks that many organisations have never previously needed to manage.

The challenge is finding a balance that allows employees to benefit from the technology without removing the boundaries that exist for good reasons.

Security controls, access models, data classification, governance processes, and organisational culture all become part of the discussion. This is not simply a technology problem. It is a governance problem.

My perspective

I suspect the future lies somewhere between unrestricted access and strict prohibition.

Organisations will increasingly need governance models that define what information can be accessed, by whom, under what circumstances, and with what level of transparency.

The discussion will extend beyond security teams and involve legal, compliance, privacy, and business stakeholders.

In many ways, the debate is less about artificial intelligence and more about information ownership, accountability, and trust. AI simply forces organisations to confront questions that already existed but were easier to ignore.

An open question

As AI systems become more capable and more deeply integrated into business processes, where should organisations draw the line between access and control?
