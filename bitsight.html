External Security Exposure

This was one of those pieces of work that I found especially interesting because it sat between technical investigation, ownership, and practical remediation. It was not just about spotting an issue. It was about understanding what externally visible findings were really showing, tracing them back to infrastructure, and working out what could realistically be improved.

Context

External security monitoring was highlighting findings across multiple environments. These included TLS-related issues, certificate problems, and patterns suggesting misconfiguration or weaker controls on publicly reachable services.

The challenge was that these findings did not automatically explain who owned the asset, what the underlying service was, or how straightforward remediation would be.

How I approached it

  • I started by reviewing the findings carefully rather than treating them as generic alerts.
  • I looked at what was actually being flagged, such as TLS versions, certificate mismatches, and self-signed certificate exposure.
  • I then worked to connect those findings back to real services and infrastructure.
  • I tried to separate what was clearly actionable from what needed more context or coordination with other teams.

What mattered most

What made this valuable was not just identifying technical weaknesses, but helping turn scattered external findings into something more structured and understandable. That meant thinking about ownership, business impact, feasibility, and how remediation would actually happen in practice.

This is the kind of work I enjoy because it combines investigation, communication, and security thinking rather than just applying a fix in isolation.

Why I include it here

I see this as a good example of how I like to work. I try to understand the real shape of a problem first, then bring structure to it, and then move it toward a solution that is technically sound but also realistic in a live environment.
